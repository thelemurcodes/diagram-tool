Survey readiness as a continuous practice

State surveys and QA audits are not one-time events that a facility scrambles to prepare for — they are snapshots of practices that should be consistent every day. That said, a structured preparation cycle in the weeks before an anticipated survey window, or before a scheduled internal audit, significantly reduces the likelihood of cited deficiencies and gives staff the confidence to demonstrate what they do routinely.

Facilities that treat survey prep as a continuous cycle — reviewing findings, implementing corrections, auditing compliance, and repeating — tend to see deficiency counts fall over successive surveys and are better positioned to respond when surveyors arrive with specific questions or requests. The workflow below describes both the annual/cyclical prep process and the real-time response when surveyors are on-site.

Step 1: Reviewing prior survey deficiencies and corrective action plans

Pulling the last survey report

Preparation starts with the most recent state survey report and any focused review or complaint-driven survey conducted since. The director of nursing or administrator reviews each cited deficiency — what was found, at what scope and severity level, and what corrective action was accepted by the state. This review answers the first and most important question: have the corrections we committed to actually been implemented and maintained?

Auditing accepted corrective actions

For each prior deficiency, the facility should be able to produce evidence that the corrective action is in place. Evidence might include: updated policy documents signed by the administrator, training records showing affected staff completed retraining, observation logs showing consistent practice, or quality monitoring data showing the problem rate has declined. If a correction was promised but the underlying practice has drifted back, that is the facility's most significant vulnerability going into the next survey.

Trending across survey cycles

Facilities that have been surveyed multiple times can look for patterns. Deficiencies that recur in the same tag area across two or more surveys signal a systemic issue — a gap in supervision, a training problem, a policy that doesn't match actual practice, or an environment factor that keeps reasserting itself. Recurring patterns get prioritized in the mock survey walkthrough.

Step 2: Mock survey and internal audit walkthroughs

What a mock survey accomplishes

A mock survey is an internal walkthrough that simulates what a state surveyor would observe, ask, and request. It is conducted by a person — ideally someone not embedded in the day-to-day routine of the unit being surveyed — who deliberately looks for gaps: an unsigned care plan, a medication not accounted for in the MAR, an unsecured medication cart, a missing monthly weight, a call light out of reach.

The value of a mock survey is not the checklist — it is the perspective shift. Staff who work in a unit every day develop blind spots. A mock surveyor looking at the environment and the documentation for the first time will often notice things that have become invisible through familiarity.

Who conducts the mock survey

Larger facilities may bring in an outside consultant for a formal mock survey once or twice a year. Smaller facilities typically rotate the responsibility internally — the DON surveys one unit while the unit manager audits another, or a peer facility exchange is arranged. Some state associations and trade organizations offer mock survey services. Whatever the mechanism, the person conducting the mock should have enough regulatory knowledge to recognize what surveyors commonly examine in each care area.

What is examined

A thorough mock survey covers: resident record completeness (care plans current and signed, assessments completed on schedule, physician orders in place, advance directives present), medication administration records (accurate, no omissions, PRN justifications documented), physical environment (life safety, infection control, resident room condition), staff practices (observed hand hygiene, PPE use, transfer technique, call-light response time), and resident and family interviews (satisfaction, knowledge of rights, any concerns not previously surfaced).

Documenting findings and assigning corrections

Mock survey findings are documented in a findings log with each gap, the responsible department or individual, a target correction date, and the person accountable for verifying completion. The log becomes the internal corrective action plan driving the remainder of the prep cycle. Items with short correction windows or high regulatory risk are addressed first.

Step 3: Resident record and MAR completeness

Care plan currency

Surveyors consistently examine whether care plans reflect the resident's current status. A care plan written at admission that has not been updated after a significant change — a fall, a new diagnosis, a hospitalization, a functional decline — is a common deficiency area. The review should confirm that every resident with a change in condition in the preceding 90 days has a documented care plan update with a new date and the responsible clinician's signature.

MAR accuracy and gap analysis

The medication administration record is one of the most frequently audited documents. A MAR review checks for: medications listed that do not have corresponding physician orders, orders on file with no corresponding MAR entry, PRN medications administered without a documented indication, missed doses without an explanation, and controlled substance counts that don't reconcile with the pharmacy dispensing record. Even a single unexplained gap in a MAR can trigger a broader medication-management review.

Assessment completion schedules

Depending on the level of care and applicable regulations, facilities are required to complete periodic assessments on a defined schedule — quarterly, semi-annually, or following a significant change. The record audit confirms that every required assessment for every resident has been completed within the required window, is legible, is signed by the appropriate clinician, and has resulted in a care plan review if indicated.

Incident and grievance documentation

Surveyors may request a list of incidents and grievances from the preceding year and pull a sample for detailed review. Each incident should have a complete report, evidence of family notification within required timelines, a documented root-cause review, and a corrective action. Grievances should show that the facility acknowledged the complaint, investigated, responded to the resident or family in writing within the required timeframe, and documented the resolution.

Step 4: Document and binder readiness

Policy and procedure binders

Surveyors routinely ask for the facility's written policies in specific areas — infection control, medication management, fall prevention, elopement, abuse prohibition, disaster preparedness. Policies should be current (reviewed and signed within the required review cycle, typically annually), consistent with actual practice, and physically or electronically accessible to the staff who are expected to follow them. A policy that exists but that frontline staff have never read is almost as problematic as no policy at all.

Training and competency records

Staff training records should document: new-hire orientation completion, annual required training topics, competency checks for specialized skills (feeding assistance, transfer technique, wound care, medication administration for licensed staff), and any retraining triggered by an incident or prior deficiency. Training records should be current for all active employees; gaps for staff hired within the past year are particularly scrutinized.

Emergency preparedness documentation

Emergency preparedness is a dedicated review area in most survey frameworks. Facilities should be able to produce: a written emergency plan covering the hazards specific to their geographic location, documented annual drills (including fire drills on each shift), a community contact list, a staff contact tree, a list of residents requiring evacuation assistance, and evidence that all required drills have been conducted and critiqued within the past year.

Licensure and certification documents

The facility's operating license, the administrator's license, nursing staff licensure verification, and any required department-of-health certificates should be current and accessible. Many surveyors check license status in real time through state databases — expired or lapsed credentials identified during a survey can result in immediate jeopardy findings.

Step 5: Staff interview preparation

What surveyors ask staff

During an unannounced survey, surveyors commonly approach frontline staff — CNAs, med techs, nurses — directly and ask open-ended questions: "If a resident fell right now, what would you do first?" "Where is the emergency call procedure posted?" "Who do you notify if a resident refuses a medication?" "What do you do if you observe or suspect abuse?" Staff who can answer these questions clearly and consistently — without looking at a binder — demonstrate that the facility's training has been internalized.

Running competency check conversations

In the preparation period, charge nurses and department heads can informally walk through these questions with their teams during shift huddles. The goal is not to script answers — surveyors recognize scripted responses — but to surface genuine knowledge gaps and fill them before the survey. A staff member who hesitates on the abuse-reporting chain or who doesn't know where the emergency exit map is located is revealing a training gap that can be addressed immediately.

Staff confidence in their rights and the survey process

Staff should also understand their own rights during a survey: they can ask for a question to be repeated, they can say they don't know and will find out, and they should not feel pressured to answer questions outside their scope or knowledge. Facilities that brief staff on the survey process — what surveyors are looking for, what a typical survey day looks like, that it is normal and expected — tend to have calmer, more accurate staff interactions during the actual survey.

Step 6: Environment-of-care rounds

Life safety

The physical environment is among the most observable areas surveyors assess because it can be evaluated without records or interviews. Life safety rounds confirm: fire suppression systems are functional and inspected, fire doors close and latch properly, exit signage is illuminated and unobstructed, corridors and exits are clear of stored items, emergency lighting functions, and fire extinguishers are current on inspection. Any citation in this area carries significant regulatory weight because of the direct relationship to resident safety.

Infection control environment

Infection control rounds look at: hand hygiene product availability at every point of care, appropriate separation of clean and soiled supplies, PPE availability and proper storage, food safety practices in the kitchen and dining area, laundry handling procedures, and the overall cleanliness and condition of resident rooms and common areas. Facilities that recently experienced an outbreak should expect focused review of the isolation procedures and the actions taken in response.

Resident room and common area conditions

Resident rooms are examined for: call light within reach of the resident, bed at the lowest safe position, personal belongings treated with respect and in accessible locations, sufficient lighting and ventilation, and absence of trip hazards. Common areas are checked for accessibility, cleanliness, and the availability of resident amenities consistent with the facility's stated programming. Dining observations may include meal temperature, positioning of residents who need assistance, and the ratio of staff available to assist with feeding.

Common deficiency areas

Care plan currency

Care plans not updated after significant changes in condition, hospitalization, or functional decline — one of the most consistently cited areas across facility types.

Medication management

MAR gaps, missing PRN justifications, unreconciled controlled substance counts, and medication storage or labeling issues.

Abuse prohibition

Inadequate screening at hire, missing or incomplete abuse training records, and failure to report or investigate allegations within required timeframes.

Infection control

Hand hygiene gaps observed by surveyors, improper PPE use, inadequate outbreak response documentation, and food safety lapses.

Resident rights

Residents unable to name their rights, grievance processes not communicated or documented, and dignity and choice issues observed during meal or care routines.

Emergency preparedness

Missing or overdue drills, emergency plans not updated after facility changes, and staff unfamiliar with evacuation assignments or shelter-in-place procedures.

Step 7: Corrective action plan process after prep findings

When your mock survey or internal audit finds gaps

The purpose of the internal prep process is to find gaps before the state does — so discovering issues during a mock survey or record audit is a success, not a failure. Each finding should be entered into a corrective action log that captures: the specific gap identified, its likely regulatory area, the immediate interim action taken (if any), the root cause, the full corrective action plan, the target completion date, and the name of the person responsible for verification.

Root cause, not symptom treatment

A corrective action that addresses only the visible symptom — filing the missing progress note, replacing the expired policy — without examining why the gap occurred will not hold. Root cause analysis asks: Was there a supervision gap? A training gap? A policy that staff don't know about or can't access? A workload issue that caused the step to be skipped? A communication breakdown between departments? The corrective action is sized to the root cause, not the symptom.

Monitoring and sustainability

Once a correction is implemented, it should be monitored through the quality assurance process to confirm it is sustained. A weekly audit of a corrected practice for the first 30–60 days, with results reported to the QAPI committee, gives the facility evidence that the correction is holding — and gives leadership early warning if it begins to drift again. This monitoring data also becomes the evidence the facility would present to a surveyor if the same issue were examined in the next survey cycle.

Connecting prep findings to QAPI

Survey preparation and the facility's ongoing quality assurance process should be connected, not parallel. Findings from mock surveys and internal audits feed into the QAPI committee's agenda, contribute to trend analysis, and inform the facility's annual quality improvement priorities. Facilities that run survey prep as a separate exercise disconnected from QAPI tend to find themselves addressing the same issues repeatedly, because the mechanism for sustained improvement is not engaged.
